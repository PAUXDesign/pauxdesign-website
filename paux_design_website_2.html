What's the difference between working with you vs a design agency?
Agencies charge for account managers, project managers and overhead you never see. You often end up with junior designers on the actual files. With me, you work directly with a senior designer on every decision — and you pay for the work, not the overhead. That means faster delivery, clearer communication, and a result that's coherent.
How long does a typical project take?
A UX audit typically takes 1–2 weeks. A full product design project runs 4–10 weeks depending on scope. A Framer website build is usually 2–4 weeks. I work in clear stages with deliverables at each step, so you always know where things stand.
How does pricing work?
I work on fixed-scope projects — so you always know what you're paying for before we start. A 50% deposit starts the project, with the balance due on delivery. Book a free call and I'll give you a clear price range within 24 hours.
What if I only need part of the process?
That's completely fine. If you already have wireframes and need UI design, we start there. If you have a design but need the Framer build, I can step in at that point. The process is a framework, not a rigid contract.
What does the free audit call actually involve?
A 30-minute video call where I look at your product or website live and give you honest, specific feedback. You'll leave with at least 3 concrete things to fix — regardless of whether we work together afterwards. No pitch, no pressure.

Enterprise Compliance & Business Management Platform

Designing a compliance-first SaaS that transforms dense legal complexity into intuitive workflows non-technical users can navigate confidently.

Overview

A SaaS platform for organisational management and regulatory compliance including GDPR and occupational health regulations. Required granular access control, strict data separation, and auditability by design.

I led the project end-to-end, translating legal requirements into product decisions — not just screens.

The challenge
"How do you translate 850 rows of Excel and dense legal documents — written for lawyers — into a system non-technical users can follow without ever making a compliance error?"
Before — problems
  • 850+ Excel rows tracking certifications manually
  • Legal documents written for lawyers, not end users
  • Manual email reminders for every deadline
  • No visibility into who approved or signed what
  • Undocumented, complex audit processes
After — vision
  • Guided workflows enforce compliance automatically
  • Legal requirements translated into step-by-step UI
  • System-triggered notifications by real-time status
  • Audit trail integrated into every action
  • Role-based access for each organisation type
Design approach
Strategy 01
Legal requirements as UX constraints

Analysed regulatory frameworks to extract mandatory processes — then designed the UX to enforce them invisibly, not through warnings.

Strategy 02
Information architecture as a safety layer

Structured navigation to prevent unauthorised actions — through structure, not error messages.

Strategy 03
Role-based mental models

Roles as configurable containers, not fixed profiles — transforming complex permission matrices into something admins could manage confidently.

Strategy 04
Guided workflows

Users couldn't take non-compliant paths — not because the interface blocked them, but because those paths simply didn't exist in the system.

Key decisions
🏗️
System-first architecture: 10 interconnected modules sharing unified logic with strict data separation — designed as a system, not a collection of screens.
🔐
Role-based access: Configurable containers rather than fixed profiles — non-technical admins could manage permissions without needing to understand the data model.
⚙️
Automated compliance workflows: Document assignment, review, approval and signature structured through predefined flows that enforce correct actor involvement.
👤
Designing for non-technical users: The UX had to provide clear mental models and guide high-stakes actions without ambiguity — embedding compliance into the product itself.
Key learnings
📖
Translating legal complexity: Reading GDPR and extracting what needs to be enforced through design — not documentation.
🔗
Systems over screens: The challenge was architecting how 10 modules share data, enforce permissions and maintain audit trails across complex journeys.
🤝
Stakeholder translation: Bridging legal experts and engineers — with design as the common language.

I specialise in turning regulatory and operational complexity into products people can actually use.

Overview

Mila AI is a language learning platform for Chinese and Japanese students. The goal was to launch a mobile-first experience and reposition AI personalisation as a visible value proposition — not a hidden backend feature.

I led the end-to-end UX and UI. The challenge wasn't adapting the web to mobile — it was rethinking how progression, personalisation and continuity should work in short, on-the-go sessions.

The challenge
"Users had to navigate multiple menus just to reach a lesson. There was no clear learning roadmap, and students lost their place after every interruption."
Problems identified
  • Multiple menus to reach a single lesson
  • No learning roadmap or visible progression
  • Users lost context after every interruption
  • AI personalisation invisible to users
  • UI designed for desktop, not mobile sessions
Client goals
  • Modernised UI for current mobile standards
  • Clear communication of AI personalisation
  • Experience adapted for short, on-the-go sessions
  • Seamless continuation between sessions
Design strategy
Strategy 01
Mobile-first architecture

Rebuilt IA around quick access and clear progression. Fewer steps to the lesson, clearer sense of where you are.

Strategy 02
Personalised onboarding

Users define goals and level on first launch. The system adapts immediately, creating ownership from day one.

Strategy 03
Adaptive learning roadmap

A dynamic roadmap that unlocks based on progress and makes AI-driven adaptation visible — turning AI from backend logic into a feature the user can see.

Strategy 04
Resume & continuity

Progress saves automatically. "Continue Learning" is the primary action. Users re-enter without losing context — one tap to resume.

Outcome
✅
85% usability improvement in structured testing with early users.
✅
Improved clarity in progression — users knew where they were and what to do next without guidance.
✅
AI adaptation perceived as visible — users reported the app felt like it was "learning them", not serving generic content.

Building an AI-powered product?

Making AI feel useful and visible to non-technical users is one of the hardest UX challenges. It's one I specialise in.

JobLink — Multi-Profile Job Search App

Redesigning job search for the 43% of people who apply across two or more industries — but have only ever been offered one career identity.

UX Designer (end-to-end)
Scope

Platform

Overview

JobLink lets users manage multiple career profiles and apply across industries without switching accounts. The key insight: 43% of job seekers apply across 2+ industries but every platform assumes one career identity.

I ran the full process — user research, competitive analysis, card sorting, wireframing, A/B testing and high-fidelity iOS UI.

The problem
"Users were spending 15+ hours a week just customising applications. Many gave up on roles they were qualified for — it was simply too much work."
User pain points
  • Creating multiple accounts (against platform policies)
  • Rewriting profiles for every industry
  • One-size-fits-all CV that stands out nowhere
  • 15+ hours/week lost to customisation
  • Abandoning qualified roles — too much friction
Research findings
  • 59% wanted multiple career profiles
  • 70% passively job hunting — time is scarce
  • 98% search for jobs near their location
  • Users want quick-scan cards, not descriptions upfront
  • Profile switching mental model already known (Instagram)
Design process
Phase 01
User research & surveys

Key insight: the biggest pain wasn't finding jobs — it was the time cost of tailoring each application.

Phase 02
Competitive analysis

Analysed Indeed and TotalJobs. Gaps: no multi-profile support, poor tracking, no distance search — these became JobLink's differentiators.

Phase 03
Card sorting & IA

Organised into four sections: Home, Applications, Messages, Profile. Flowcharts and wireframes to validate navigation before visuals.

Phase 04
A/B testing

My assumption — detailed descriptions upfront — was wrong. Users wanted quick-scan cards to filter fast. This changed the entire IA.

Impact on behaviour
Before JobLink
  • 3 resumes in Google Drive, manually managed
  • 20+ minutes per application, customising everything
  • Often abandoned mid-application — decision fatigue
After JobLink
  • 2 profiles created once, one-tap apply
  • 5 jobs applied in the time it used to take for 1
  • Users reported feeling "in control" of their search
Key learnings
🔍
Design for the exception, not the average: Identify the underserved edge case and design for them first — career changers, multi-skilled professionals.
🧪
A/B testing proved my assumptions wrong: Always test before committing to an IA decision.
✂️
Reducing cognitive load beats adding features: Profile switching and one-tap apply were loved. Smart suggestions were ignored. Simplicity is the feature.
